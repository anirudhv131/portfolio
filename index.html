Academic Research

Market Design

Price Accuracy and Resolution Capture in Prediction Markets

This paper distinguishes a prediction market's trading layer, which sets the price, from its resolution layer, which determines the outcome, and argues that the accuracy attributed to these markets belongs only to the former. Using 2,100 resolved Polymarket markets, I find prices are well calibrated while documented resolution failures concentrate in markets with ambiguous settlement rules.

Behavioral Finance

Does Retail Attention Predict Returns? Evidence from Robinhood "Popularity" Data, 2018 to 2020

This paper examines whether changes in retail investor attention, measured through Robinhood ownership data from 2018 to 2020, contain predictive information for subsequent returns. I assess whether attention-driven buying forecasts performance or instead coincides with periods of overvaluation.

Sports Economics

What Predicts World Cup Success?

This paper investigates which national characteristics, including economic resources, population, and domestic league strength, predict success in the men's World Cup. I find that several intuitive predictors have limited explanatory power once other factors are accounted for.

Housing Policy

Reforming Credit Access: Addressing Structural Exclusion in Urban Homeownership

This paper examines how the design of credit scoring contributes to structural exclusion from urban homeownership, even absent explicit discrimination. I review the mechanisms through which these effects arise and evaluate potential reforms to credit access.

Long Warner Bros. Discovery (NASDAQ: WBD): Arbitraging the Paramount Deal

This is a merger-arbitrage position, long Warner Bros. Discovery into Paramount's signed $31.00 all-cash offer. It is not a view on the media business. The position pays out if the deal closes, and that outcome has little to do with where the market goes. WBD trades near $26.95, so the spread to the $31 offer is about 15 percent, which is wide for a deal this far along. The DOJ cleared it in June, along with China and Australia, and what is left is mostly a threatened lawsuit from a group of state attorneys general, who rarely stop a merger the federal agencies have already passed. A $7 billion break fee and a recent rival bid from Netflix limit how far the stock would fall if it broke. The shareholder vote and an expected third-quarter close are the near-term catalysts.

Equity Research · Pair Trade · Jun 20, 2026

Long Texas Roadhouse, Short Cava

This is a market-neutral paired position that holds Texas Roadhouse long and Cava short in equal dollar amounts, so it makes money only if Texas Roadhouse outperforms Cava regardless of where the sector goes. The case rests on a split in the 2026 consumer. Texas Roadhouse posted 7.1% comparable sales growth on 4.5% traffic growth in Q1, which is the better kind of comp because it comes from more guests rather than price alone, and it still trades about 10% below its 52-week high. Cava, meanwhile, has run roughly 54% in six months and now trades well above what the business currently earns, at the exact moment the premium end of the restaurant market faces the most consumer pressure. Both companies report Q2 results in August, with Cava due on August 11, which is the main near-term catalyst for the spread.

Equity Research · Jun 15, 2026

The Cigna Group (NYSE: CI): A Healthcare Giant Mid-Transition

Cigna has become the subject of unusually wide disagreement on the Street, with one bank raising its price target to $400 and another cutting to $302 off the same set of facts. This report builds a discounted cash flow model from Cigna's segment-level financials and historical cash conversion, arriving at a $319 price target, roughly 12% above where the stock traded at the time of writing. The thesis is that the market is treating a pharmacy-benefit pricing shift and two business divestitures as pure downside when the underlying cash generation tells a different story.

Simulator

A Monte Carlo laboratory for geometric Brownian motion, the price process behind Black-Scholes. Terminal prices are drawn from the exact solution of the SDE, so the only error is sampling error, and every run is checked against the closed-form lognormal benchmark. Runs are seeded, so any result here can be reproduced.

Method notes. Each terminal price is drawn from the exact solution S(T) = S(0)·exp((μ − σ²/2)T + σ√T·Z), so there is no discretization error, only Monte Carlo error, which shrinks at rate 1/√N. The drawn paths above use the same exact transition applied step by step, which is valid because GBM increments are independent and lognormal. Random numbers come from a seeded Mulberry32 generator through a Box-Muller transform, so a given seed always reproduces the same run. Theoretical percentiles use Acklam's rational approximation to the inverse normal CDF (absolute error below 1.15e-9). One thing worth noticing as you move the sliders: for large σ or long T the mean pulls far above the median, because the lognormal is right skewed, and the probability of finishing below the start can stay high even when the average outcome looks strong. The average path is not the typical path.
